I moved to Tokyo in August 2025. Before that, I was living in New York City, working as a software engineer, making good money, surrounded by close friends, and generally comfortable. Then I packed up and flew 14 hours to start over in a country where I could barely read a restaurant menu.

Eight months later, I have some thoughts.

## Why I Came

I'd been interested in Japan for a long time — the culture, the language, the food, the general vibe of a place that feels like it was designed by people who actually care about public spaces. I got an offer from a Japanese tech company called Hennge, working as a frontend engineer out of their office near Shibuya. The role was interesting, the location was a dream, and I figured: if not now, when?

So I said yes, sold or stored most of what I owned, and moved to a small apartment near Shinanomachi station.

## The Daily Rhythm

My commute is Shinanomachi to Shibuya — a short, painless train ride that I genuinely enjoy. Tokyo trains are everything people say they are: clean, on time, quiet. Coming from the NYC subway, it feels like traveling in a different century.

I go into the office three days a week. The other two I work from home or from a cafe somewhere. Tokyo has an unbelievable number of good cafes, and most of them don't care if you sit there for hours with a laptop as long as you buy something every now and then.

Outside of work, I've built a life around the things I love. I boulder at climbing gyms around the city (and recently injured myself doing it, because of course). I've gone skiing at places like Naeba and Hakuba — resorts that are shockingly accessible from Tokyo by train. I've spent way too many evenings at arcades. I've wandered through Kinokuniya bookstores for hours. I've eaten food that has permanently ruined me for most other cities.

The small things add up. Conbinis that actually have good food. Streets that are clean at 2 AM. The fact that you can leave your bag at a table in a coffee shop, walk away for ten minutes, and it'll still be there. It sounds cliché until you live it every day.

## Working in Japanese Tech

This is where it gets complicated.

Hennge is a good company. The people are smart and kind. But working in Japan as a software engineer — especially coming from the US — involves a cultural adjustment that nobody fully prepares you for.

The biggest difference is communication style. Japan is a high-context culture. People don't say what they mean directly; they imply it, hedge around it, leave space for you to read between the lines. In America, if something is broken, you say "this is broken, here's how we fix it." In Japan, that same directness can come across as aggressive or disrespectful. I've had to learn to frame problems as questions, propose solutions humbly, and resist the urge to just be blunt about things.

Self-promotion is another adjustment. In the US, advocating for yourself is expected — you quantify your impact, you talk about what you shipped, you make sure people know your contributions. In Japan, that reads as arrogant. The culture values collective credit and quiet dedication. It's a fundamentally different operating system for career growth, and it's one I'm still figuring out.

Then there's compensation. I'll be real: the pay gap is significant. I took a substantial pay cut to come here. Software engineering salaries in Japan are a fraction of what they are in the US, even at good companies. The whole market is anchored lower — a combination of decades of wage stagnation, seniority-based pay systems, and a corporate culture that prioritizes stability over aggressive compensation. It's the single hardest thing to reconcile, especially when you're used to the American tech salary structure.

## Learning Japanese

I came here with basic Japanese and a vague plan to "get better." Eight months in, I can navigate daily life — ordering food, buying things, basic conversations. But real fluency feels far away.

Japanese is genuinely hard. Three writing systems, thousands of kanji, a grammar structure that's basically the inverse of English, and a level of politeness encoding in the language itself that adds layers of complexity. I study regularly — SRS for kanji and vocab, extensive reading, trying to consume media in Japanese. I'm working toward the JLPT N2 exam, which feels ambitious but necessary.

The hardest part isn't the study itself. It's the constant low-grade frustration of not being able to fully express yourself. I can order ramen without breaking a sweat. I cannot explain a nuanced technical problem to a coworker in Japanese, and that gap is humbling every single day.

On the other hand, every small win — understanding a sign without thinking, catching a joke in a conversation, reading a few sentences of a game without reaching for a dictionary — feels like real progress. It's slow, but it's happening.

## The Honest Pros and Cons

**What's great:**

- **Quality of life.** Tokyo is one of the best cities in the world to actually live in. The infrastructure, the food, the safety, the cleanliness, the public transit — it's world-class in ways that are hard to appreciate until you experience them daily.
- **Access to nature.** Skiing in Hakuba, hiking in the mountains, climbing at outdoor crags a couple hours from the city. Japan packs an absurd amount of natural beauty into a small country, and the train system makes it all accessible.
- **The culture of care.** People here care about doing things well. The barista cares about your coffee. The train conductor cares about being on time. The convenience store clerk cares about how they bag your stuff. It's a level of conscientiousness that permeates everything.
- **Adventure.** Living abroad is inherently interesting. Every day involves small discoveries — a new neighborhood, a hidden shrine, a vending machine selling something inexplicable. The novelty hasn't worn off.

**What's hard:**

- **Money.** The pay cut is real and it affects everything. Saving is difficult. The things I used to impulse buy — nice computer parts, clothes I liked — now require more deliberation. It's not that Tokyo is expensive (it's actually cheaper than NYC in many ways), but the salary difference more than offsets the lower cost of living.
- **Distance from friends.** My close friends are in NYC, and a 14-hour time zone difference makes staying connected harder than I expected. You can't just hop on a quick call when you're awake at opposite hours. The loneliness creeps in.
- **Language barrier.** Even in a city as foreigner-friendly as Tokyo, not speaking the language fluently creates a ceiling on how deeply you can integrate. Workplace conversations, bureaucracy, making Japanese friends — it all hits different when you're operating at 30% of your communicative capacity.
- **Career trajectory.** The Japanese tech market doesn't reward experience the same way the US market does. Seniority-based systems, lower pay bands, less aggressive career progression — it can feel like you're trading years of career momentum for the experience of living abroad. Whether that's worth it depends on what you value, but it's a real trade-off.

## So, Would I Do It Again?

Yes. Without hesitation.

Not because everything is perfect — it's clearly not. The financial reality is frustrating. I miss my friends. Some days I just want to go home. But I came here to do something hard and different, and that's exactly what this has been.

I've learned things about myself that I couldn't have learned staying comfortable in New York. I've developed patience I didn't know I needed. I've built a life in a language I'm still learning, in a culture that operates on entirely different assumptions than the one I grew up in. That's worth something that doesn't show up in a bank account.

I don't know how long I'll stay. Maybe another year. Maybe I'll head back to NYC eventually. But right now, I'm here, and I'm glad I came.

If you're thinking about making a similar move — do it. Just go in with your eyes open. It's not a vacation. It's a real life, with real trade-offs. And it's one of the best decisions I've ever made.
